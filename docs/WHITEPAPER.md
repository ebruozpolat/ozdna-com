## Abstract

Generative AI collapsed the cost of producing images; it did not collapse the cost of trusting them. From August 2, 2026, the EU AI Act's Article 50 requires providers of generative AI systems to mark AI-generated content in machine-readable form, with California's SB 942 taking effect the same day. Most of the thousands of small GenAI applications in scope have nothing built.

**OriginDNA** is content provenance infrastructure that turns this obligation into one API call. Every image gets a "DNA": (1) a **cryptographic signature** attached at creation, following the open C2PA standard; (2) a **timestamp anchored in a public, append-only record** that no one — including us — can backdate; and (3) a **perceptual fingerprint** stored in a registry, so that a copy whose metadata has been stripped by a social platform can still be matched back to its origin record.

The architecture is token-optional and no token is planned. Revenue is conventional SaaS. v1 signs images only (JPG/PNG); video, audio and mobile capture are explicitly out of scope until the core is proven.

## 1. The problem: provenance dies in transit

Two failures define the current landscape.

**First, verification lags generation.** Detection-based approaches — classifiers that guess "is this AI?" after the fact — are structurally losing: error rates carry real harm (photographers have been disqualified from competitions over false AI accusations), and the compute economics are unsustainable even for philanthropically funded nonprofits (TrueMedia, the best-known detection nonprofit, shut down in January 2025). OriginDNA deliberately contains **no AI detection**. Provenance works from the other end: content proves where it came from, at creation, cryptographically.

**Second, provenance metadata does not survive the places content actually lives.** The C2PA standard solves signing; it does not solve transit. Major social platforms still strip C2PA metadata on upload, while a growing set of surfaces (LinkedIn's CR badge, YouTube's capture labels, Google's "About this image") *display* credentials when present. RAND's 2025 analysis documents this stripping problem as the central weakness of metadata-only provenance. A signature that vanishes on upload protects no one.

The C2PA specification itself (v2.4, April 2026) anticipates the answer: third-party manifest repositories and a published Soft Binding API — mechanisms for re-attaching provenance to content that has lost its metadata. Today the only live implementation is vendor-run and images-only. **No neutral, API-first registry exists.** That unoccupied position is OriginDNA.

## 3. Design principles

1. **Provenance, not detection.** No classifiers, no "is this AI?" guesses. Content carries its own evidence.
2. **Interoperate with the standard; never overclaim it.** OriginDNA signs to the open C2PA standard. Until we complete the C2PA Conformance Program, our signatures validate cryptographically but display as "unknown source" in the official Verify tool — so v1 ships its **own public verify page** and never promises "trusted Content Credentials." Honesty about this is a feature: a provenance company that overclaims its own trust status has no product.
3. **Neutrality is the moat.** Not tied to one creative-tools vendor, one platform, or one model provider. API-first, standards-based, designed for the registry role the spec anticipates but no neutral party fills.
4. **Verification requires no account and no trust in us.** Anyone can recompute a fingerprint and check the public record. If OriginDNA disappeared, the proofs would still stand.
5. **Token-optional; no token planned.** See §7.
6. **Never touch user funds.** No custody, no wallets holding user value, no trade execution — by architecture, not policy.
7. **Client pays the compute where possible.** Free-tier signing runs in the user's browser (WASM); the marginal cost of a free user approaches zero.

## 4. Architecture: sign, anchor, remember, verify

### 4.1 Sign

Images are signed at creation with C2PA manifests. The free flow signs **in the browser** via WASM (`@contentauth/c2pa-web`) — the client's device does the work, which is what makes a genuinely free tier sustainable. Paid API tenants use metered server-side signing compute, priced into their tier. Key management is treated as first-class from day one: a single key-handling vulnerability was enough to force a major camera maker to revoke an entire signing program.

### 4.2 Anchor

Content hashes are **batched** and anchored to a public, append-only record at a benchmark cost of ~$0.0001 per asset. Batches trigger on thresholds (queue size / age / paid-item priority), with a published service level: anchored within 24 hours on the free tier, within 1 hour for paid tenants. The design is chain-agnostic; the anchoring wallet is ours and holds only a small gas budget — never user funds. This is the entire "blockchain" story: a boring, neutral timestamp mechanism that anyone can independently check.

### 4.3 Remember — the DNA registry

At signing time, the registry stores the content's cryptographic hash **and two perceptual fingerprints** (a 64-bit DCT perceptual hash and PDQ-256). When a platform strips metadata, re-encodes, resizes, or a user screenshots the image, the perceptual fingerprints still match the altered copy back to its origin record — signature, timestamp, anchor proof and all. This is the piece that platform-level signing initiatives do not solve, and it maps directly onto the EU Code of Practice's "fingerprinting backed by registry databases" mechanism. The registry exposes a match endpoint; adopting TrustMark (the open-source, C2PA-approved watermark) alongside fingerprinting is under evaluation so the registry speaks the standard's own soft-binding language.

### 4.4 Verify

A public page: drop a file, get the provenance record — who signed it, when it was anchored, the anchor proof, and a match confidence for stripped copies. Verification verdicts use deliberately conservative language and never imply trust-list status we do not hold.

**v1 scope:** images only (JPG/PNG). Out of v1: video, audio, mobile capture apps, detection of any kind.

## 5. The registry thesis

Signing tools are becoming commodities — the incumbent creative-tools vendor gives creators free signing, and major model providers are rolling out joint watermark-plus-metadata systems for their own outputs. What no one operates is the **neutral memory layer**: the place where provenance survives transit and where any verifier — platform, newsroom, court, marketplace — can ask "have you seen this content before, and what do you know about it?"

The registry compounds: every signed image makes the match service more useful, and every match request makes registration more valuable. It is infrastructure in the literal sense — most valuable when boring, neutral and permanent. That is also why the fundable milestone is **paying API customers**, not registry size: the registry's value is proven by verifiers relying on it, not by raw counts.

## 7. Token stance: token-optional, none planned

The whitepaper states this plainly because the category's history demands it. The three best-known attempts to attach tokens to provenance businesses ended in a −99.9% all-time low (June 2026), a −98% token followed by a rebrand away from the original thesis within 16 months, and a silently abandoned token with a now-dormant company. In each case the token destroyed credibility without funding the business.

OriginDNA's architecture does not require a token for any function: signing is cryptography, anchoring is paid in gas by us, the registry is a database with an API. If the registry ever evolves into a decentralized attestation network, that becomes a legitimate question **later** — decided through a proper legal wrapper, funded by capital that exists by then. It is not part of this design, this roadmap, or this company's revenue model. In 2026, *no token and real revenue* is the differentiated position.

## 8. Business model & sustainability

Conventional SaaS. The cost structure is deliberately asymmetric: free-tier signing costs us approximately nothing (client-side compute), anchoring is fractions of a cent per asset amortized across batches, and paid tiers carry metered server-side compute priced at a healthy margin. Fixed infrastructure targets a near-zero monthly budget on serverless primitives. Non-dilutive funding (ecosystem and data-infrastructure grants) is the first external capital target, applied for with a shipped product; the milestone that makes the company fundable is paying API customers.
