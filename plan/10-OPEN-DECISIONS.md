## Read this first (for the founder)

These are the open calls and checks that stand between the plan and a working, legal product.

**Most of them are your dev lead's to make, not yours.** You are not expected to answer the technical ones — nobody expects a distribution-and-PR founder to know whether an image fingerprint comes out identical in two places. What you *are* on the hook for is different and just as important:

**You are the person who must make sure every one of these gets answered before the build week that depends on it.** A check that should happen in August, discovered un-done in October under deadline, is how the whole launch slips. Your job with this document is to (1) hand it to your dev lead at kickoff, (2) ask them to put a date next to each item, and (3) chase the few that need a lawyer or a signature from you.

How to use it:

- **Sections A–C and E** are your dev lead's calls. Skim them so you understand the shape of the risk, then let them own the answers.
- **Section D** is the lawyer/accountant list. Some items say **[LAWYER]** — those are not a dev's call and not yours to guess. The plan already flags that roughly **$300–500 of Turkish legal counsel is justified before launch**; this is the list to spend it on.
- **"Stays YOUR call"** near the end is the short list of business decisions you must *not* hand to the dev lead.
- The **copy-paste checklist** at the very end is written so you can read the questions aloud in your first conversation with the dev lead.

A note on how to read the tables: "If it is gotten wrong" is the worst realistic outcome, not a prediction. Most of these will be fine — the point of listing them is that a few won't be, and we want to find those in a cheap week (August/September) rather than an expensive one (launch).

## A. Verify early — before or in Week 1

## B. Design decisions the dev lead makes

These are choices, not tests — the dev lead decides, ideally records the reasoning, and some are one-way doors that are painful to change later. A few marked *(both)* touch pricing or trust wording, so loop the founder in.

| Item | What it is (plain language) | Why it matters | If it is gotten wrong | Where in the plan |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **B1. Timestamp vs. silent signature expiry (~2029)** *(both — copy)* | If we can't attach a trusted timestamp at signing time, our in-file signatures quietly stop validating when our certificate expires (~2029) — and no normal test catches it before then. Decide: attach one, or make marketing say the in-file proof is time-limited (the blockchain anchor is the durable backstop). | Our whole pitch is "proof that survives." A silent future expiry contradicts that, so it's a trust-critical *copy* decision driven by the A6 spike. | In-file proofs silently break years later, or we overclaim a durability we don't have. | 01-ARCH §9.6; 02-TECH §2 |
| **B2. Shared vs per-customer official certificate** *(both)* | If we ever join the official C2PA trust program, the authorities only issue *bottom-level* certificates. Forces a choice: one shared official cert for everyone (loses per-customer isolation) or one per customer (a recurring per-cert fee nobody has priced). The pre-build email to c2pa.org must ask that price. | It changes both our security-isolation model and our cost model, and the price is unknown and unbudgeted. | A future architecture surprise plus an unbudgeted recurring cost, or a design that can't cleanly become conformant. | 01-ARCH §9, §12 |
| **B3. Post-hack "which old signatures still count" logic** | When a signing key is compromised, we decide which past signatures still count by comparing *when the blockchain timestamped it* against *when the key was compromised*. Signatures proven to exist before the compromise stay trusted. Must be implemented precisely. | This is the anti-Nikon story and a literal pitch slide ("a later hack doesn't destroy your old proof"). | Wrong logic either invalidates good signatures or trusts compromised ones — the core differentiator becomes unreliable. | 01-ARCH §9, §12 |
| **B4. Fingerprint "band" layout** *(one-way door)* | To search fingerprints fast, each is split into fixed "band" columns. How many and how wide — plus "how different can a copy be and still match" — is one coupled choice that's expensive to change: altering it later means rebuilding the whole table on live data. | It directly controls how well stripped/edited copies are found, and can't be changed cheaply once data accumulates. | Either poor matching quality, or a painful, risky full-table migration later. | 01-ARCH §4.4 |
| **B5. Do free users stay anonymous or must they log in?** *(both)* | Two parts of the plan disagree: sign anonymously (with a one-time deletion token) or log in first. Affects sign-up friction (marketing) *and* how much personal data we store (privacy). The MVP-spec doc is meant to be the tiebreaker but it's still open. | It changes conversion on the flagship free flow and our data-minimization posture at the same time. | Needless friction that kills free adoption, or storing more personal data than intended. | 01-ARCH §5, §3.1, §8 |
| **B6. Keep or swap the manifest-reading library** | The library that reads an image's embedded signature on the verify page says in its own docs it's still under development and its trust-chain checking is incomplete. Acceptable for v1 (we check against our own registry), but the keep-or-replace call is left open and must be closed. | It backs the verify page's "signature present and valid" verdict. Cheaper to decide before build than after. | The verify page mis-reports a signature as valid or invalid. | 04-MVP §8 Week 2 |
| **B7. What the "signature valid" badge may claim** *(both — trust wording)* | Whose digital identity sits on a signature, whether each paying customer gets their own certificate or all share one company cert, and therefore the exact wording of our strongest trust claim. | This wording *is* the core trust claim of a trust product, and it drives the signing architecture everything else keys off. | If the badge claims more than the cryptography proves, we overclaim — a legal and reputational hazard; a wrong handshake assumption means rebuilding the signing feature. | 05 §T11 |
| **B8. Chain-agnostic schema from day one** | Build the database and code so the underlying blockchain can be swapped without a rewrite — a column recording which chain was used, plus a thin abstraction layer. Base is run by one company; its fees/policies could change, or "crypto" could become a PR liability. | Cheap insurance now (a schema choice) versus an expensive forced migration later; also a grant-strategy neutrality talking point. | Getting welded to one chain means a painful, risky migration exactly when a fee hike or outage forces the issue. | 05 §T10 |

## C. Security review — before anything goes live

The whole product sells trust, so these are the failure modes a professional audit would target first. None of these should ship unreviewed. Items marked *(both)* have a physical-custody or legal edge the founder shares.

| Item | What it is (plain language) | Why it matters | If it is gotten wrong | Where in the plan |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **C1. Root signing keys created + kept fully offline** *(both)* | The master keys that all trust flows from must be created and stored completely offline — USB sticks and paper, never on any internet-connected machine. The founder physically holds these backups. | This is the crown-jewel secret. If the root key ever leaks there is no fix — every OzDNA signature ever made becomes untrustworthy. | Total, unrecoverable compromise of the entire signing hierarchy. | 01-ARCH §9; 03-ALG §5.1–5.4 |
| **C2. Per-customer signing keys encrypted under one master key** | Each paying customer gets their own signing key. To fit free limits, all those keys are locked inside the database with a single master "wrapping key" held in Cloudflare's secrets vault — which is still in beta. That one key + the beta vault are single points of failure; needs review and a fallback plan. | If the wrapping key or the beta vault is mishandled, *every* customer's signing key could be exposed at once. | Mass compromise of all customers' keys, or a beta service failing with no tested fallback. | 01-ARCH §9; 09-DEV §5 |
| **C3. Key ceremony + rotation/revocation runbook** *(both)* | A three-tier key chain (offline master → monthly-use → day-to-day signing), per-customer certs minted in an offline ceremony, 90-day rotation, and a written "what to do if a key leaks" plan. The founder should not improvise the offline ceremony alone. | Nikon lost its entire signing program to one key leak. This is basic hygiene that regulated buyers ask about — not having it is disqualifying for the compliance wedge. | A key leak with no rehearsed rotation turns a manageable incident into an existential one, with no playbook. | 03-ALG §5.1–5.4; 05 §T6, §T11; 08 §5 item 6 |
| **C4. Signing endpoint locked against abuse** | The endpoint that signs images is a tempting target: if someone makes it sign whatever they want, they produce fakes stamped "signed by OzDNA". Defenses: require an account, cap free signs, add a bot-check, and log every signature so abuse is traceable and revocable. | An abused signing endpoint lets attackers forge our signature onto deepfakes — the exact reputational catastrophe the whole design exists to prevent. | Fake "OzDNA-signed" content in the wild destroys the trust the entire business sells. | 03-ALG §4.2 |
| **C5. Gas wallet isolated; never touches user funds** *(both — legal line)* | A tiny wallet pays the few cents per blockchain timestamp. Its "hot" key lives only in the background anchor job (unreachable from the public site); an offline "recovery/rotator" key can swap it if stolen. It must hold only ~$20 of *our own* money and *never* receive or move any customer money — in Turkey, unlicensed handling of user funds is a criminal offense. | It's both a security boundary and a legal red line the founder is personally exposed to (Law 7518). | A leaked hot key lets a thief spam junk entries; any drift toward holding user money is criminal exposure for the founder. | 01-ARCH §6, §8; 02-TECH §7; 03-ALG §3.5, §5.2; 08 §5 item 7; 09-DEV §10; hard rule 2 |
| **C6. Anchor job never double-submits after a crash** | The job that writes batches to the blockchain must never send the same transaction twice — wasting gas or corrupting the batch sequence — if it crashes and restarts. The safe-restart (idempotency) logic must be correct. | Getting restart/retry wrong wastes money or breaks the anchor-record integrity the entire verify story rests on. | Double-spent gas, corrupted batch numbering, or records silently left un-timestamped. | 01-ARCH §4.2 |
| **C7. Hand-written contract + Merkle code: test vectors + independent review** | The ~50-line blockchain program and the "combine many fingerprints into one receipt" code are hand-written from scratch, and the contract holds real money and includes a "rotator" recovery key. Build to spec with fixed, committed test cases, plus automated analysis and *one independent human review* before it goes live. | A subtle bug in hand-rolled crypto or in the contract's access control could make proofs unverifiable or let the wrong key control anchoring. A clean review is also a sales asset. | Proofs that don't verify for anyone, a drained wallet, or an anchor contract an attacker can misuse. | 02-TECH §7; 01-ARCH §5; 06 §3, §8 |
| **C8. "Verify by URL" hardened against SSRF** | One verify mode lets a user hand us a web link and we fetch that image. If not fenced, attackers can trick our server into fetching internal/private addresses. Allow only public addresses, image types, a size cap, and a short timeout. | A weak fetch feature is a classic way for attackers to probe our internal systems through our own server. | Server-side request forgery — a genuine security breach vector. | 04-MVP §4.4, §9.4 |
| **C9. Login + API-key + webhook security** | Logins use one-time email links; API keys are shown once and stored only as a scrambled hash (so we can't leak them even if the database is stolen); revocation is instant; incoming payment webhooks and outgoing event notifications are cryptographically checked so nobody can forge "this customer paid". | This is the front door to every account and paid feature; a forged "payment succeeded" grants free access, a forged "refund" blocks a paying customer. | Leaked/non-revocable keys or forged webhooks mean account takeover, billing fraud, or forged records. | 04-MVP §4.1; 09-DEV §5 |
| **C10. Abuse/spam defenses on every free surface** | The scarce resource isn't compute (users' browsers do that) — it's our daily database write budget and the registry's trustworthiness. Defenses: per-IP + per-key caps, bot-check (Turnstile), cap of ~20 new accounts per IP/day, verify email before registering, bulk uploads paid-only, an abuse-report button, and a way to flag/kill bad records. ~90–120 hostile free accounts could saturate the daily write budget. | Weak controls on a free, anonymous flow can exhaust quotas, poison the registry (which degrades the moat's matching), or blow the budget. | Registry spam, quota exhaustion mid-day, or cost blowout from a determined abuser. | 01-ARCH §7, §3.1; 04-MVP §4.6, §7; 05 §T5; 09-DEV §5 |
| **C11. Public verify page hardened against forged matches** | Anyone can hit the "check this image" page repeatedly to reverse-engineer a fake that fools it (a known, documented attack). Add rate limits, a bot challenge, return vague "high/medium similarity" (not exact scores), and log suspicious query patterns. | The verify page is the product's public face; a "the proof site blessed my fake" demo at launch is essentially unrecoverable for a trust brand. | A public forged-match demonstration collapses the product's core promise. | 05 §T4 |
| **C12. Backups proven by a real restore drill** *(both)* | The registry database *is* the company and can't be rebuilt from the blockchain alone. Needs: automated nightly export stored **off** Cloudflare, a weekly copy pulled to the founder's own machine, and an actually-rehearsed full restore. Plus alerts if the anchor job silently fails for days or the wallet runs low. Cloudflare's built-in "Time Travel" is **not** a backup — it dies if the account dies (its retention window is also unconfirmed). | "The registry is the moat." Losing it, or discovering backups don't restore, or not noticing a week of failed anchoring, would each be catastrophic and hard to spot. | An untested backup is often a broken backup; a failed restore after data loss ends the company. | 01-ARCH §10; 05 §T8; 08 §5 item 8 |

## E. Watch at scale / later

These don't block launch — but if they pass unnoticed the product degrades silently *exactly as it starts succeeding*. The upside: hitting these triggers means the business is working. Put them in an operations runbook and monitor.

| Item | What it is (plain language) | Why it matters | If it is gotten wrong | Where in the plan |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **E1. Registry-size cliffs** | Cloudflare's free database (D1) holds ~2M records and covers year one. Known cliffs: ~2M → move to the $5/mo paid tier; ~10M → switch matching to the stronger fingerprint's index; ~40M → outgrow D1 entirely. Also tighten match thresholds as the registry grows so results stay accurate. Not decided now, but must be written down and watched. | If these pass unnoticed, deep searches get slow or infeasible and false matches creep up. | Matching performance, cost, or accuracy degrades silently. | 03-ALG §1.5, §2.5–2.6; 06 §2; 05 §T3; 08 §4.3 |
| **E2. Fingerprint-matching index scaling** | Answering "which stored fingerprint is closest to this new image?" gets slow and CPU-heavy as the registry grows into millions — you can't scan the whole table each time. How to index that search, and at what size the basic DB stops coping, is an open engineering decision. | The verify/match feature is the core moat; if it can't scale, the headline capability degrades as it succeeds. | Verify queries time out or balloon in cost at scale, forcing an urgent redesign under load. | 06 §2; 05 §T3 |
| **E3. Free-tier daily write cap** | The free database accepts only 100,000 new writes/day; each registration is many writes, so a burst or a single high-volume customer can hit the cap. Know the break point and have the paid-tier move ready before it bites. | It sets the moment we must start paying and the volume we can't serve for free. | Past the cap, new registrations silently fail — losing the very provenance records customers pay us to store. | 01-ARCH §2; 08 §4.3 |
| **E4. Data retention / archival policy** *(both)* | Every image ever registered keeps costing a little storage every month, forever — free-forever users included. Decide how long data is kept, when to move old records to cheaper storage (that cheaper price is itself unverified — check before relying on it), and how this squares with the legal duty to delete on request. Policy must exist by launch. | It's the one cost that compounds over time, and it intersects the legal deletion duty (D1). | Unbounded storage creep, and a gap between "keep forever" and the legal duty to delete personal data on request. | 06 §4.1, §6 |
| **E5. Budget triggers stay inside the ceiling** | Budget is near zero. The plan commits $5/mo for Cloudflare's paid plan from launch and holds a $20/mo option in reserve *only* if the browser/server signing approach fails. Confirm these triggers and flag early if a costly fallback looks likely. | Keeps spend inside the ~$20/mo ceiling and avoids a surprise jump if a signing fallback is triggered. | Budget overrun, or discovering too late that a costly fallback path is needed. | 02-TECH §16, §1; 01-ARCH §3.2 |
| **E6. Server CPU cost per paid image** | For paying API customers, *our* servers decode and fingerprint each image (guessed 50–200ms). This is the only realistic dent in the ~90% gross-margin story. Measure early and keep watching as volume grows. | If the guess is badly low, margins and the per-image planning number are wrong. | Understated serving cost erodes margins at scale. | 06 §2, §4.1 |
